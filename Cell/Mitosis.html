Mitosis

DEFINITION

Mitosis is the type of cell division that occurs when there is a need for growth or repair of tissue. The number of chromosomes in daughter cells is the same as in the parent cell, which is why it is also called Equational division. One single parent cell divides into two identical daughter cells. The stages of mitosis are called "Phases".

PHASES OF MITOSIS

Interphase

Interphase is the longest stage of the cell cycle, occupying nearly 90–95% of a cell’s life. During this time, the cell is not actively dividing but is metabolically very busy. It grows in size, produces proteins, and carries out its normal functions such as energy production and synthesis of molecules. This stage is crucial because it prepares the cell for the upcoming division.

Interphase is divided into three sub-stages: G1, S, and G2. In G1, the cell grows and builds organelles. In the S phase, DNA replication occurs, doubling the genetic material so that each chromosome now consists of two identical sister chromatids. Finally, in G2, the cell continues to grow and produces proteins specifically needed for mitosis, while also checking for DNA damage. This ensures the cell is ready for division.

Prophase

Prophase marks the beginning of visible changes in the cell. The chromatin fibers condense into distinct chromosomes, each consisting of two sister chromatids joined at the centromere. This condensation makes the genetic material easier to move during division. At the same time, the nucleolus disappears, signaling that the cell is shifting its focus from normal activity to division.

As prophase progresses, the nuclear envelope begins to break down, allowing spindle fibers to interact with the chromosomes. Centrosomes, which organize the spindle apparatus, migrate to opposite poles of the cell. The spindle fibers extend toward the chromosomes, preparing to attach to their centromeres. This sets the stage for proper chromosome alignment in the next phase.

Metaphase

In metaphase, chromosomes align along the equatorial plane of the cell, known as the metaphase plate. This alignment is critical because it ensures that each daughter cell will receive an identical set of chromosomes. The spindle fibers attach to the kinetochores at the centromeres of the chromosomes, securing them in place.

The metaphase stage also serves as a checkpoint. The cell verifies that all chromosomes are properly attached to spindle fibers and aligned correctly. If errors are detected, the cell halts division until corrections are made. This safeguard prevents unequal distribution of genetic material, which could lead to mutations or cell malfunction.

Anaphase

Anaphase is the shortest but most dramatic phase of mitosis. The centromeres split, and the sister chromatids are pulled apart by the spindle fibers. Each chromatid is now considered an independent chromosome. Motor proteins and microtubules work together to move these chromosomes rapidly toward opposite poles of the cell.

This separation ensures that each daughter cell will inherit an identical set of chromosomes. The equal distribution of genetic material is the defining feature of mitosis, distinguishing it from meiosis, where genetic variation is introduced.

Telophase

Telophase is essentially the reverse of prophase. The chromosomes, now at opposite poles, begin to de-condense back into their less visible chromatin form. This allows the cell to resume normal functions after division. The nuclear envelope reforms around each set of chromosomes, creating two distinct nuclei within the cell.

The nucleolus reappears, and the spindle fibers disassemble. At this point, the cell has two nuclei, each genetically identical to the parent nucleus. The cell is almost ready to complete division, with only the cytoplasm left to divide.

Cytokinesis

Cytokinesis is the final step of cell division, where the cytoplasm splits to form two separate daughter cells. In animal cells, this occurs through the formation of a cleavage furrow, which pinches the cell into two. In plant cells, a cell plate forms in the center, eventually developing into a new cell wall that separates the two daughter cells.

Once cytokinesis is complete, the two daughter cells enter interphase and resume their normal cellular activities. Each cell is genetically identical to the parent cell, ensuring continuity of genetic information across generations of cells.
